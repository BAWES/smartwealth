Fixed Income
Bonds are investments in debt issued by governments, agencies, or corporations globally. Typically, we categorize our bond investments into two categories: U.S. Bonds and Non-U.S. bonds. While they share similar characteristics to U.S. bonds, international bonds offer exposure to different geographies and currencies. While they generally offer lower returns than stocks, bonds are less volatile. Bonds also tend to move inversely to stocks, providing stabilization during periods of volatility.

Developed Market Stocks
Developed Market Stocks are an ownership share in foreign companies in developed economies like U.S., Europe, UK and Japan. U.S. Stocks act as the core asset class because history demonstrates that they offer significant returns over the long run. Foreign stocks provide indirect exposure to currency fluctuations. The historical returns of foreign stocks are lower than U.S. stocks and have higher risk, so the main impetus for investing in foreign stocks are their diversifying qualities.

Emerging Markets Stocks
Emerging Markets Stocks are an ownership share in foreign companies in newly developing economies like Brazil, Russia, India and China. Emerging Markets Stocks offer additional diversification because these investments respond differently than foreign developed economies. The historical returns of Emerging Markets Stocks are higher than U.S. Stocks, with higher risk. Emerging Markets add the possibility of higher returns to a portfolio and additional diversification.

Real Estate
Real Estate are an ownership in Real Estate Investment Trust (REITs) ETFs in developed markets. A REIT is a type of security that invests in real estate through property or mortgages and often trades on major exchanges like a stock. REITs provide investors with an liquid stake in real estate. They receive special tax considerations and typically offer high dividend yields.
